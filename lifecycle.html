The Life Cycle of a Grape

Dormancy to Harvest

Winter

Dormancy: From leaf fall to the beginning of growth in spring, grapevines are dormant and consist entirely of woody tissue. Relatively little activity occurs during this period.

April

Bud Break: The first tender buds of the growing season emerge from the dormant vine during bud break. It is the first stage of the vine cycle that yields grapes each fall.

May

Flower Emergence: Inflorescences begin to swell, and the buds begin to open. They are extremely fragile in this stage.

June

Flowering: Grapevines bear greenish flower clusters, so small that they are hardly noticeable.

July/August

Fruit Development: Most grapevines self-pollinate, after which fruit set occurs. Even if the variety of grape is red, you'll see small green clusters. This clusters are hard at first.

August

Verasion: The colorful moment when those hard, green grapes transform into plump, juicy clusters. Veraison is especially noticeable in red grapes as the fruit turns from green to purple. In white grapes, the clusters turn from bright green to a more mellow golden green. (This is my favorite stage!)

September/October

Harvest: The winemaker or vineyard manager decides when to pick dependent upon sugar and acidity levels, and weather measured by the Refractometer.
